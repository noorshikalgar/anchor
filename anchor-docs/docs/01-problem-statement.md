# 01 — Problem Statement

## The problem, in plain language

A person in their late 20s has too many self-improvement goals competing for the same
few hours: sleep, diet, gym, reading, learning to code, hair/skin care, and just
keeping up with email and messages. They know *what* to do. What they don't have is:

1. **A way to decide what to work on first**, instead of trying everything at once
   and burning out in a week.
2. **A plan that survives real life** — living in a joint/extended family in India
   means schedules routinely get overridden by relatives visiting, family
   obligations, shared living spaces, and work that doesn't respect a 6pm cutoff.
   A missed night of sleep or a skipped gym day currently feels like "I failed,"
   which snowballs into abandoning the whole plan.
3. **Something to make the weekly decisions for them** — not just log what
   happened, but actually say "here's what to focus on this week, here's what
   changed from last week, here's the smallest version of each habit for a bad day."

## Why existing habit-tracker apps don't solve this

Research into the current top apps (Streaks, Habitica, Habitify, Loop, Productive,
Way of Life, Strides, Notion-based setups) shows they cluster around two
philosophies: **minimalist streak-counting** (tap a circle, don't break the chain)
or **comprehensive gamification** (XP, avatars, RPG mechanics, social parties).
Both groups share the same blind spots for this person's situation:

- **They track habits, they don't sequence them.** Almost none of them tell you
  *which* habit to start first or when to add the next one — the person is
  expected to already know their own prioritization.
- **They treat every missed day the same way** — a broken streak, a lost XP bar,
  a sad character. There's no concept of "this was an unavoidable disruption
  (guests, deadline, travel) vs. a lapse in discipline," and no built-in recovery
  path.
- **They assume a stable daily context.** None of the research surfaced a habit
  app designed around living with extended family, shared spaces, or
  culturally-specific interruption patterns common in Indian households.
- **"AI" in existing apps is usually just smarter reminders** (e.g. WhatsApp
  pings instead of push notifications), not actual weekly planning or
  prioritization help.
- **Gamification and social pressure can backfire** for someone who is already
  dealing with a long, slightly overwhelming list of self-improvement goals —
  it adds another thing to manage (a pet, an avatar, a guild) rather than
  reducing decision load.

## The actual gap Anchor fills

Anchor is not "another habit tracker." It is a **weekly decision-support system**:
it decides (with the person, not for them, but doing the heavy lifting) what to
focus on this week, sequences habits sensibly, defines a fallback "minimum viable"
version of each habit for disrupted days, and treats disruption as an expected
input to the plan rather than a failure state.

## Non-goals

- Not a social/community app. No parties, leaderboards, or public streaks.
- Not a generic to-do list or calendar replacement.
- Not a medical/nutrition app — it won't give clinical diet or sleep-disorder advice.
- Not trying to cover every possible habit category on day one — see the MVP scope.
